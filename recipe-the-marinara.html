From Scratch
Recipe No. 2 —

The Marinara

Time 60 Min
Serves 10
Season All
Difficulty Easy

Why this one

For it's simple beauty.

Ingredients

All You need

— A  #10 Can of Alta Cucina Whole Peeled Tomatoes
6-10 cloves of garlic (garlic pressed)
2 small Sweet (or Yellow) Onions w/small dice
1/2 Cup Of Olive Oil (maybe more)
— Salt & Pepper
1 cup of basil chopped or torn

Method

Before You Start

Use a pot deep enough that it could hold twice the tomatoes so you have enough room to stir and break them up without making a mess, unless you enjoy being messy. Put it on a burner on low-medium heat to warm half your olive oil.

01

Take your garlic that's been put into a garlic press and add it to the oil. You could mince it, but dare to press it. Make sure you have your onions diced too. With a wooden spoon stir the garlic with the oil and let the fragrance really have a good time with you.

02

As the garlic transforms into golden essence put your onions in the pot. Add the other half of your oil if it seems like it needs it. This oil is the magic to the onions, garlic, and marriage about to happen.

03

As the onions become somewhat translucent, it's a message to your tomatoes, "Come in please." Let the tomatoes join the party. They can be any whole peeled tomatoes, but they are truly better off being Alta Cucina tomatoes. I can't even tell you why exactly, so don't ask, just discover for yourself.

04

Turn the heat to medium-high and take a whisk and stir periodically. As the sauce begins to simmer, turn the heat to medium and let time and your whisking ways anoint the world.

05

Your stirring will involve some mashing. Use the whisk to mash the tomatoes and help break them up naturally as you whisk. You'll do this for 30-60 minutes.

06

There's gotta be more to it, right? You really can't add too much olive oil. So you can add some more now, or just later when you use your marinara for a dish. But a little salt and pepper here is a nice touch. And don't forget the basil. Turn off your sauce and just fold it in with the whisk. At least one cup. And you're done.

Notes

For Another Day

Sometimes I'll make a double batch of sauce and put it in large mason jars. Seal them hot with this nifty companion so there's no need for a water bath, and let cool to room temperature on the counter. (Follow long practiced canning methods, many suggest a dash of citrus, or citric acid powder, to preserve in a pantry for a year.) Or just keep it as is (I don't use citrus) and store in a refrigerator for months just fine as long as it's sealed.

Note

Just when you think something is always one way, dishes like this remind us fresh isn't always what you think it is or may imagine it to be. May this refresh your day into something newly discovered.
